Officer Roles & Responsibilities

If you're preparing for an upcoming Toastmasters meeting role, you're in the right place! See below for all the ins and outs of your role, any resources you need, and tips curated by Vanderbilt Toastmasters to help you succeed at your next meeting!

For extra resources, be sure to review Toastmasters International's guides for meeting roles.

Toastmaster

As Toastmaster, you serve as the meeting's director, host, and emcee. Additionally, you select the week's meeting theme.

Your role involves working with the VPE and General Evaluator to ensure all club participants know their responsibilities for the week, introducing speakers during the club meeting (reach out to them before the meeting to get their speech title, project title, objectives, delivery time, and a short bio), and ensure smooth transitions throughout the meeting.

When conducting transitions, Vanderbilt Toastmasters encourages having the Toastmaster of the Day share fun facts related to the meeting's theme to lighten the mood.

Speaker

As a Speaker, you serve as a role model for all members and guests. Your responsibilities include preparing, rehearsing, and delivering a speech during your next meeting. Additionally, you should be sure to discuss your goals, strengths and weaknesses with your evaluator prior to giving your speech.

Your speeches may be a part of the Pathways Curriculum; if you're new to Toastmasters, we recommend starting here. If you've been around for a while and are still using the legacy curriculum, feel free to continue on that path. Regardless, you can always speak to your VPE to determine the best path forward for you.

Topics Master

As Topicsmaster, you deliver the Table Topics® portion of the meeting, which helps train members to quickly organize and express their thoughts in an impromptu manner.

Before the meeting, coordinate with the Toastmaster of the Day to obtain the meeting theme and generate topics related to the theme which allow speakers to express their opinions. You should also focus on giving members who aren't assigned a speaking role during the meeting the opportunity to speak by voluntelling them to deliver an impromptu Table Topics® speech.

General Evaluator

As General Evaluator, you evaluate everything that takes place during the club meeting. Additionally, you serve as the leader for the evaluation team (Ah Counter, Wordmaster/Grammarian, and Timer).

Additional responsibilities include ensuring other evaluators know their responsibilities, explaining the purpose and process of evaluations to the meeting, and taking notes on the meeting's timeliness, enthusiasm, and organization for your General Evaluator's report at the end of the meeting.

Speech Evaluator

As a Speech Evaluator, your role is critical to producting the constructive feedback which is the heart of the Toastmasters program. Before your upcoming meeting, ask those you've been assigned to evaluate what they will present and what they wish to achieve.

During your assigned speakers' speeches, take notes so you can deliver and objective 2-3 minute verbal evaluation for them at the end of the meeting before giving them your written evaluation. We recommend following the Commendation, Recommendation, and Encouragement style of feedback.

To further prepare for your role, be sure to review Toastmasters International's Guide to Effective Evaluation.

Wordmaster/Grammarian

As Wordmaster/Grammarian, your role is twofold.

As Wordmaster, you should select an interesting word for the Word of the Week. Then, throughout the meeting, record the number of times each speaker uses the Word of the Week. Finally, at the end of the meeting, report who used the word the most and award a prize/kudos as appropriate.

As Grammarian, note any egregious uses of the English language or grammar mistakes throughout the meeting. This includes incomplete sentences, mispronunciations, grammatical mistakes, non-sequiturs, malapropisms, etc. At the end of the meeting, report these errors and how to correct them.

Timer

As Timer, you're responsible for monitoring time for each meeting segment and each speaker.

Before the meeting, be sure to obtain the timing cards from the Sergeant at Arms and know the time allotments for each speaker. At the beginning of the meeting, explain your role and what each meeting segment's time allotment is. Then, throughout the meeting, be sure to time each portion of the meeting (you can use your phones's stopwatch) and record each time so you may deliver a report at the end of the meeting for each speaker's time taken.

Ah-Counter

As Ah-Counter, your responsibility is to note any overused words or filler sounds used as a crutch by anyone who speaks during the meeting. Words may be inappropriate interjections, such as like, so, so, and you know. Sounds may be ah, um or er.

At the end of the meeting, be prepared to report of how many crutches were used by every person who spoke during the meeting. If someone uses greater than five crutches, we recommend reporting that they used greater than five rather than the exact number - those speakers can approch you after the meeting for an exact count.
